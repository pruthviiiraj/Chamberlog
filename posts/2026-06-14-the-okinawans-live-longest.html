The Okinawans live the longest and it's not about the food

Hara hachi bu isn't a diet. It's a cellular intervention.

The Japanese practice of stopping at 80% fullness is not a caloric calculation. It is a biological one. When you overeat, digestion takes longer. Extended digestion accelerates cellular oxidation, the rate at which your cells effectively burn through themselves. The wear happens at the cellular level, and it compounds over years. The people of Okinawa, one of the longest-lived populations on earth, practice this not as discipline but as deeply ingrained habit. What looks from the outside like a diet tip is actually a mechanism for running the engine at lower heat, more often, for decades.

The practical problem is that the satiety signal has a 20-minute delay. By the time you feel full, you've already passed the optimal point. The signal can't catch up in real time if you're eating at the pace most people eat. Slowing down enough that the lag doesn't mislead you is the entire practical application of the principle. Not a different food, not a supplement, not a protocol with a product attached. A different relationship to the signal that's already there.
