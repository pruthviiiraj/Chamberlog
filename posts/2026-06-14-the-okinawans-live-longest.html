The Okinawans live the longest and it's not about the food

Hara hachi bu isn't a diet. It's a cellular intervention.

The Japanese practice of stopping when you're about 80% full, practiced in Okinawa, Japan, where people live longer than almost anywhere else on earth, is not a caloric calculation. It's a biological one. When you overeat, digestion takes longer. Extended digestion speeds up the rate at which your body's cells wear out over time. The wear is invisible in the short term and compounds quietly over decades. The Okinawans don't practice this as a discipline. They practice it the way you practice saying please: so deeply ingrained it's no longer a choice. What looks from the outside like a diet tip is actually a mechanism for running the body's engine at a lower heat, more often, for a lifetime.

The practical problem is that the signal telling you you're full has a 20-minute delay. By the time it arrives, you've already passed the optimal point. That's what happens when you eat at the pace most people eat. Slowing down enough that the signal can actually inform you before you've already overshot it is the entire practical application. Not a different food. Not a supplement. Not a protocol with a product attached. A different relationship to a signal that's already there.
