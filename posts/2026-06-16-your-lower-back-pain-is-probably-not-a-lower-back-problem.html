Your lower back pain is probably not a lower back problem

Most lower back pain is not a lower back problem. It is a hip mobility problem that the lower back is being forced to solve.

The body runs on a simple organizational rule: every major joint has a primary job, and that job is either to move or to hold. The hip is built to move, to flex, extend, rotate, abduct, and load in multiple directions. The lumbar spine is built to hold, to maintain position and protect the spinal cord while everything around it does the actual moving. These roles are not arbitrary. They follow from the structure of the joints themselves.

When a "hold" joint is stable and a "move" joint is mobile, movement works. But the moment a "move" joint gets stiff, tight hip flexors, restricted hip rotation from too much sitting, the body does not stop and ask permission. It borrows capacity from the nearest joint that will give it. That joint is almost always the lumbar spine. Suddenly the lower back, which is supposed to be stable, starts flexing and rotating to compensate for what the hip won't do. Load that movement pattern under a deadlift or a squat and the lumbar spine is now under shear force it was never designed to handle. That is where the injury comes from.

This pattern repeats itself all the way up and down the body. Ankle immobility forces the knee to rotate. Thoracic stiffness forces the shoulder to compensate. Scapular instability forces the shoulder joint to work without a stable base. In each case, the site of pain is the joint doing the compensating, not the joint that failed. Treating the pain without fixing the restriction upstream will produce the same injury again.

The broader principle here is worth sitting with. The skeleton already knows which structures should move and which should hold. The axial skeleton, your spine and rib cage, is a stability platform. The appendicular skeleton, your arms and legs, is the movement machinery. The rule is the same at every scale: the stable platform has to hold its position before the moving parts can do their job well. A shoulder press is really a thoracic mobility test. A squat is really a hip and ankle mobility test with a core stability test layered on top. If the foundation gives, the movement falls apart, and eventually something breaks.

Fix the restriction first. That is where the problem actually is.
