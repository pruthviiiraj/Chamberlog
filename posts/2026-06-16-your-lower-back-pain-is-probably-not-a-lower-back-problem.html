Your lower back pain is probably not a lower back problem

Most lower back pain is not a lower back problem. It is a hip mobility problem that the lower back is being forced to solve.

The skeleton is organized around a strict division of roles. Some joints are built primarily for stability — they hold firm, transfer force, and resist unnecessary movement. Others are built primarily for mobility — they generate range of motion and drive movement. The lumbar spine, your lower back, is a stability joint. Its job is to remain controlled while force flows through it. The hip is a mobility joint. Its job is to produce the range of motion that allows you to hinge, rotate, and absorb impact. These roles are not interchangeable. When a mobility joint fails to do its job, the nearest stability joint compensates by producing movement it was never designed to produce. The lower back, asked to generate the rotation and flexion that restricted hips can't provide, takes on repeated stress in a structure built to avoid exactly that.

This is the compensation pattern. Tight hips move less. The lumbar spine moves more to make up the difference. The spine was designed to transfer load stably, not to generate range of motion repeatedly. Do this enough times — in the gym, in the office, bending to pick things up — and the accumulation of movement in a structure designed for stability produces pain, inflammation, and eventually injury. The same pattern appears one joint higher: a stiff thoracic spine (mid-back, which should be mobile) forces the lumbar spine to compensate upward, and restricted shoulder mobility forces the thoracic spine into more rotation than it should take on. Each joint that fails its role passes the load to its neighbor.

The mistake in treatment follows directly from misdiagnosis. If the pain is in the lower back, the instinct is to treat the lower back: stretch it, strengthen it, mobilize it. This occasionally helps. More often, it doesn't, because the lower back is not the source of the problem. It is the site where the problem shows up. The restriction is upstream. Working on hip mobility — hip flexor stretches, 90/90 hip rotations, pigeon pose, couch stretch — removes the demand the hips were offloading onto the spine. Fix the restriction, and the compensation disappears along with it. The pain in the lower back was only ever the symptom. The hip was the cause.

This matters most before you have pain, not after. The compensation pattern builds slowly across thousands of repetitions. By the time it becomes a clinical problem, significant load has already accumulated. Maintaining hip mobility as a regular practice is not rehabilitation. It is the reason rehabilitation becomes unnecessary.
