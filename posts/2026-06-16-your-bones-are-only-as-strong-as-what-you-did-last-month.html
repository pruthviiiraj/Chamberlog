Your bones are only as strong as what you did last month

Every week you stop loading your bones, they get slightly thinner. That is not a metaphor. It is exactly what happens to astronauts in microgravity, people with limbs in casts, and anyone who stops exercising for an extended period.

Bone is a living material. It has sensor cells embedded through its matrix that detect mechanical pressure. When you run, lift, or hit something repeatedly, micro-stresses form. Those sensor cells detect the pressure through the way fluid moves through the bone matrix under load, and they send a signal: build more here. Specialist cells called osteoblasts respond by laying down new bone tissue, denser and better aligned to resist the specific direction of the stress. Other cells clear away the old, weaker material. The net result is that the bone gets structurally stronger at the exact site where the stress was applied.

This is Wolff's Law, named after the 19th century surgeon Julius Wolff who described it. And it has one feature most people don't fully appreciate: it is reversible, and it reverses fast.

When the stress is removed, the body reads that as a signal that the strong bone is no longer necessary. Why maintain expensive material that isn't being used? Astronauts in space have no gravitational load pressing through their skeletons, and they lose measurable bone density within weeks. A limb in a cast experiences the same thing. The bone thins not because something is wrong, but because the body is efficiently eliminating what it believes is excess structure.

The practical consequence is that bone density is not something you bank once and live off. It is maintained by continuing to load the specific sites you want to keep strong. A tennis player's hitting arm has considerably denser bone than their other arm because the serve creates high load at exactly those sites repeatedly over years. That density does not come from nutrition alone or from general fitness. It comes from the specific stress applied to that specific location.

For long-term health, this matters more than most training advice suggests. Squats and lunges are not just leg exercises. They are the primary tools for loading the spine and hips, the two sites most commonly fractured in osteoporosis. The best time to start building bone density at those sites is early adulthood. The second best time is now.

The bones you have at 60 are largely the product of what you loaded in the decades before. Every period of inactivity is not a pause. It is a withdrawal from an account you cannot open again easily.
