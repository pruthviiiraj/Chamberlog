Your Body Already Told You How to Train It

Every joint in your body was built to move in specific directions. Your elbow opens and closes like a door hinge. Your shoulder rotates in every direction like a ball bearing in a socket. Training them both the same way is like using a screwdriver as a hammer. It works briefly, then something breaks.

The deeper problem is that most people do not know what their body is actually doing when they train. The cartilage inside your knee has no blood supply. Zero. It gets all its nutrition from a fluid that only moves when you move. Take a step and the pressure squeezes nutrient-rich fluid into the cartilage like pressing a wet sponge. Stop moving and the delivery system shuts down. That "stiff" feeling after sitting for hours is not rust. It is starvation.

This changes what a warm-up actually is. Those 5 minutes of light movement before heavy loading are not a ritual. They are feeding the cartilage. High-rep, unloaded joint circles squeeze synovial fluid into the joint surfaces, plumping them up before you add weight. Skip this step and you are loading tissue that is nutritionally depleted.

Then there is the question of how deep to squat, how far to stretch, how much range to chase. Most people assume the answer is "more." It is not. Your bones set the ceiling. The depth of your hip socket, the angle of your femoral neck, the orientation of the pelvis all vary from person to person. Some people have shallow sockets that allow a full-depth squat. Others have deep sockets where the femur hits the rim at parallel. No amount of stretching changes where bone meets bone.

The signal to look for is simple. If the limiting sensation is a pull on the back side of the joint, that is muscle tension. Keep working. If it is a pinch on the front side, that is compression. Bone hitting bone or bone pressing into capsule. Stop pushing and change the angle. This one distinction, tension versus compression, prevents more joint injuries than any mobility program.

The body already knows how to move. Every bone shape, every joint type, every cartilage surface is an answer to a specific engineering problem. Long bones are levers. Flat bones are shields. Hinge joints flex and extend. Ball-and-socket joints rotate in every plane. The anatomy is not a mystery to be solved. It is an instruction manual to be followed.
