The Honey Stops Working

Tolstoy told a story about a man in a well. A beast chased him in from above. A dragon waited below with open jaws. He grabbed a branch growing from the wall and hung there. Two mice, one black and one white, gnawed steadily at the base of the branch. Day and night. While he hung, he spotted honey on the leaves and licked it. For years the honey tasted good. Then one day he looked down and saw the dragon clearly. After that, nothing tasted the same.

That is the mechanism behind every midlife crisis, every 3am panic, every moment where a successful person looks at everything they built and feels nothing. It is not that the honey changed. It is that the person changed. Once you truly register that you are going to die, every distraction you used to avoid that fact loses its power. You cannot un-see the dragon. And you cannot go back to licking honey the same way.

Tolstoy mapped out four escape routes. Ignorance: just do not think about it. Hedonism: enjoy the ride and ignore the drop. Suicide: the logical exit if nothing matters. Weakness: see the truth, lack the nerve to act, keep going out of inertia. He tried them all. None of them resolved the question. They only delayed it.

Then he wrote a novella about a man named Ivan Ilyich, who did everything society told him to do. Studied law. Married well. Got promoted. Decorated his house. Every decision followed the script. He never asked whether any of it was actually his. Then he fell from a ladder while hanging curtains and it killed him. He died for decoration. For the impression. For things he wanted only because he saw other people wanting them.

On his deathbed, Ivan was surrounded by people performing concern. His colleagues thought about promotions. His wife thought about the pension. The only person who did not lie to him was Gerasim, a peasant servant who held his legs for hours and said simply: "We shall all come to it some day." That single honest sentence did more than every other relationship in Ivan's life. Gerasim could be present because he had already accepted death. Everyone else was still pretending it only happened to other people.

The thing that kept Ivan stuck was not the dying. It was his own story about why his life was correct. He followed the rules. He was proper. Every time the truth approached, his mind recoiled. Only when compassion for his son cracked that story did the light appear. Not because the light was new. Because the wall came down.

This is the part Tolstoy kept circling back to. Death is not the enemy. It is a sculptor. It removes what does not matter until only truth, love, presence, and humility remain. The honey actually tastes better after you see the dragon, because now you are tasting it on purpose instead of using it as anaesthesia.

Every morning is borrowed. Every conversation is happening on a branch being gnawed by mice. That is not a morbid thought. It is the only thought that makes you pay attention.
