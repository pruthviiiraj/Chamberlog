The problem isn't the pain. It's expecting it to resolve.

Most people don't suffer from sadness. They suffer from the fear of sadness.

There are two kinds of hard problems, and confusing them is one of the most expensive mistakes a person can make. Complicated problems can be solved: they have parts, mechanisms, and with enough work you can crack them. Complex problems cannot. Not because the algorithm hasn't been found yet, but because the problem is alive. A marriage is complex. Grief is complex. Your relationship with your own ambition is complex. These can only be lived inside of. They resist engineering at every level. The category error is treating complex problems as if they were complicated: applying a dating app to the mystery of falling in love, offering frameworks for "processing" grief as if it were a troubleshooting guide you could run to completion, trying to optimize your way out of loneliness. The fix is not to stop solving complicated problems. It is to stop expecting complex problems to yield to the same tools.

When something painful arrives, the brain registers the threat before the pain has fully landed. So most of the energy goes into anticipatory resistance: trying to avoid, numb, or escape the feeling before it settles. The result is not that you experience the sadness cleanly. You experience it twisted up with dread, avoidance, and shame about having it at all. This is what makes suffering feel unbearable: not the pain, but the fighting against the pain. When you stop resisting and recognize the pain as part of a real life rather than an interruption to the life you were supposed to have, the suffering doesn't disappear, but it changes character. It becomes information rather than threat. The most meaningful periods of a person's life are almost always accompanied by the greatest negative emotion. Suffering is not the opposite of a good life. It is one of its primary ingredients.

Naval Ravikant noticed something that connects here from a completely different angle: fulfillment doesn't require acquisition. The achiever who gets what they wanted and the person who stopped craving both arrive at the same internal quiet. What they share is the same stillness. One path requires continuous results. The other requires nothing at all. If your calm depends on how things turn out, it will always be provisional. The peace you're trying to reach by completing everything on the list doesn't live at the end of the list.
