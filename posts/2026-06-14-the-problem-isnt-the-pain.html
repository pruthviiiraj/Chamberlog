The problem isn't the pain. It's expecting it to resolve.

Most people don't suffer from sadness. They suffer from the fear of sadness.

There are two completely different kinds of hard problems, and confusing them is one of the most expensive mistakes a person can make. Complicated problems can be solved. They have parts and mechanisms, and with enough work you can crack them. Think of a broken engine or a maths problem. Complex problems can't be solved in the same way. Not because the right approach hasn't been found yet. Because the problem is alive. A marriage is complex. Grief is complex. Your relationship with your own ambition is complex. These can only be lived inside. They resist engineering at every level. The category error is treating complex problems like complicated ones: applying a dating app to the mystery of falling in love, offering frameworks for "processing" grief as if it were a troubleshooting guide you could run to completion, trying to optimize your way out of loneliness. The fix isn't to stop solving problems. It's to stop applying the optimization tool to things that aren't optimization problems. The fighting doesn't just fail. It generates suffering of its own.

When something painful arrives, the brain registers the threat before the pain has fully landed. So most of the energy goes into bracing against it: trying to avoid, numb, or escape the feeling before it settles. The result isn't that you experience the sadness cleanly. You experience it twisted up with dread, avoidance, and shame about having it at all. This is what makes suffering feel unbearable: not the pain, but the fighting against the pain. When you stop resisting, when you recognize the pain as part of a real life rather than an interruption to the life you were supposed to have, the suffering doesn't disappear, but it changes character. It becomes information rather than threat. The most meaningful periods of a person's life are almost always accompanied by the greatest negative emotion. Suffering isn't the opposite of a good life. It's one of its primary ingredients.

The same mechanism runs through the way people chase fulfillment. Naval Ravikant, a tech founder who has written extensively about how the mind works, noticed something that connects here: fulfillment doesn't require acquisition. The person who gets exactly what they wanted and the person who stopped craving both arrive at the same internal quiet. They share the same stillness. One path requires continuous results. The other requires nothing at all. If your calm depends on how things turn out, it will always be provisional. What's creating the gap isn't that fulfillment is far away. The pursuit itself, the fighting and chasing, is what's keeping you from it.
