You've been measuring meditation wrong

A restless, distracted meditation session is not a failed session. It is often the most productive kind.

The mistake is measuring meditation by state — how calm or focused you felt during the sit. This is understandable but wrong. A session where you felt peaceful and present doesn't necessarily mean anything changed. It may just mean you were in the right mood that morning. The research on what meditation actually does to the brain makes a different distinction: state change versus trait change. State change is what happens during the session — the calm, the focus, the clarity. Trait change is what happens between sessions — whether the brain's default patterns have shifted over time. These two things do not correlate the way people assume. You can have profound states without any trait change. And you can build genuine trait change through sessions that felt scattered and frustrating. The restless session where you kept losing focus and returning, over and over — that's training. Each return is a repetition. Each time you notice the distraction and redirect, you're building the neural pathway that makes noticing easier next time. The difficulty is the workout.

The more useful diagnostic is to check what state your brain is in before you sit, not during. If you sit down already calm and centered, the session will feel good but will train relatively little. If you sit down distracted, anxious, or mentally scattered, the gap between where you are and where you're trying to be is large. That gap is resistance, and resistance is what creates change. This is why meditating on difficult mornings — when you least want to — tends to compound faster than meditating on easy ones. The brain is most trainable when its default pattern is being interrupted most forcefully. A useful reframe: the quality of a session is determined by how much redirecting you had to do, not by how serene the experience was.

This shifts the measurement entirely. The question isn't "did I feel calm?" It's "did I keep returning?" One is a report on your mood. The other is a report on the actual work.
